Teaching Philosophy

Mathematics is a way of thinking — not a list of steps to memorize.

My classroom rests on three commitments: meet every student where they are, build intuition before formal notation, and use frequent low-stakes checks to catch misconceptions before they harden.

As a graduate SI leader and teaching assistant, I built a daily quiz-and-feedback loop that surfaced exactly where students were struggling, then adjusted instruction in real time. That approach lifted course retention by 37% and cut drop-offs to just 2%.

Having applied mathematics and analytics to real enterprise problems at TCS and First American, I can finally answer the question every student asks — “when will I ever use this?” — with stories from the field.

Foundations First

Every advanced concept is anchored to the fundamentals students already own, so nothing feels like magic.

Data-Informed Instruction

Quick daily checks tell me who needs what tomorrow — I teach the room in front of me, not a script.

Student-First Mentorship
